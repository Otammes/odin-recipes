Pasta Bolognese

Description

Pasta Bolognese: An Italian Icon

Often called the world's most beloved comfort food, Pasta Bolognese is much more than just "meat sauce." In its authentic form, it is a thick, complex, and slow-cooked ragu that celebrates the rich culinary traditions of Northern Italy.

The Origins

The dish hails from Bologna, the capital of the Emilia-Romagna region. While meat-based sauces have existed in Italy for centuries, the first documented recipe for a "Bolognese" sauce dates back to the late 18th century. Originally, it was a luxurious dish reserved for the wealthy, characterized by finely chopped veal and the absence of tomatoes.

A Brief History

The recipe evolved significantly throughout the 19th and 20th centuries. As tomatoes became a staple in Italian cooking, they were integrated into the base, though sparingly compared to Southern Italian sauces. In 1982, the Accademia Italiana della Cucina officially registered the "authentic" recipe with the Bologna Chamber of Commerce to protect the dish’s heritage from global "dilution."

Ingredients

  • Pasta (Tagliatelle or Spaghetti)
  • GroundBeef
  • Ground Pork (or Pancetta)
  • Onion
  • Carrot
  • Celery
  • Garlic
  • Tomato Paste
  • Crushed Tomatoes
  • Dry White or Red Wine
  • Beef Stock
  • Whole Milk
  • Olive Oil
  • Butter
  • Salt & Black Pepper
  • Nutmeg
  • Parmesan Cheese

Preparation

  1. Sauté the Soffritto:

    Finely dice the onion, carrot, and celery. In a large Dutch oven or heavy pot, melt a knob of butter with a splash of olive oil over medium-low heat. Add the vegetables and sauté until soft and translucent. Stir in the minced garlic and cook for just one minute more until fragrant.

  2. Brown the Meats:

    Add the ground beef and ground pork (or pancetta) to the pot. Increase the heat slightly and cook, breaking the meat apart with a wooden spoon. Cook only until the meat has lost its raw pink color; avoid over-browning to keep the texture silky and tender.

  3. Deglaze with wine:

    Pour in the dry white or red wine. Simmer while scraping the bottom of the pot to release any flavorful bits. Continue cooking until the liquid has mostly evaporated and the sharp scent of alcohol is gone.

  4. The Milk & Nutmeg:

    Pour in the whole milk and add a pinch of nutmeg. Simmer gently, stirring occasionally, until the milk has been absorbed. This classic step tenderizes the meat and balances the acidity of the tomatoes.

  5. Build the Sauce:

    Stir in the tomato paste and cook for 2 minutes to deepen its flavor. Add the crushed tomatoes and enough beef stock to just cover the meat. Season lightly with salt and black pepper (you will adjust this later).

  6. The Slow Simmer:

    Turn the heat down to the lowest setting. Cover the pot partially and let the sauce simmer for at least 3 hours. Stir occasionally, adding a splash more beef stock if the sauce becomes too thick. The goal is a rich, thick ragu where the fats have separated slightly from the sauce.

  7. Finish and Serve:

    Taste the sauce and add more salt or pepper if needed. Boil your pasta (Tagliatelle or Spaghetti) in salted water until al dente. Toss the hot pasta directly into the sauce, adding a splash of pasta water if needed to help it cling. Serve immediately with a generous dusting of Parmesan cheese.
